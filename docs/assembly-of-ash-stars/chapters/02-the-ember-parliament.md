# The Ember Parliament

The star had been dead so long that its corpse had become constitutional.

Ember Parliament hung inside the hollowed heart of the giant like a city built to be seen by no one except those who understood what institutions cost. Layers of black lattice and gold-lit chambers floated within the vast red shell. Transit shafts descended through former solar matter now frozen into transparent ribs. Defensive screens crossed the interior in faint planes, visible only when one of the arriving fleets brushed them with sensor attention. Everywhere Cael looked, architecture declared the same ancient ambition: let brilliance quarrel, but make it quarrel in public.

The `Mnemonic Dart` entered on a parliamentary lane marked by ember beacons and silent weapon locks.

Hundreds of other arrivals were already nested in the star's interior. Some came as ships large enough to carry their own weather. Some came as needle-thin diplomatic forms trailing machine swarms like ceremonial cloaks. One delegation from a lineage Cael did not immediately recognize had arrived as a string of illuminated spheres connected by equations visible in vacuum. All were heirs. All were dangerous. Every one of them represented a people that no longer existed except as compressed will and the claim to return.

Cael let his humanoid body reassemble in the docking transept and stepped out beneath a vault lined with extinguished banners. Their fabrics had decayed centuries ago, but the memory fields that described them still hovered in faint afterimage. Dead colors. Dead pledges. Dead states keeping enough form to judge the living remains of history.

"Security posture?" he asked internally.

"Parliamentary denial webs active," said the `Mnemonic Dart`. "A direct fleet action here would be possible but ruinously expensive. Which is to say everyone will behave beautifully until they decide not to."

Cael moved into the central procession.

He passed delegations that smelled faintly of ionized metals, cold salt, dust, old computation. One intelligence carried itself in a body of articulated glass full of stormlight. Another wore the shape of a compact cathedral walking on twelve mirrored limbs. At a fork in the corridor a Pelagic Calculus envoy paused beside a pool suspended in null-gravity, sending quick ripples through the water to consult its own distributed layers.

When it turned, Cael recognized Neralis-of-Tides from archive briefings and from a handful of prior low-stakes negotiations conducted across safer centuries.

She wore a humanoid outline only as a courtesy to corridor architecture. Beneath the outline her body remained fluid, a column of clear blue matter webbed with silver cognition. Her face formed as he approached, elegant and patient, as if patience itself were a weapon she had refined for geological time.

"Cael Veyr," she said. "I had wondered whether the first authenticated courier would belong to your civilization."

"And I had wondered whether yours would arrive already counting everyone's hidden reservoirs."

"Already? No. Quietly, yes."

The faintest shift of her mouth signaled amusement. With Neralis, humor always looked like an agreement waiting to become binding.

"Did the Furnace Crown intercept you as well?" Cael asked.

"They attempted to classify me on approach and then discovered classification requires consent if the target is old enough."

"Useful lesson."

"One Varkast appears unwilling to learn."

They continued toward the chamber together. The act was noticed immediately. In a parliament of heirs, even the direction two envoys chose to walk could be read as a draft treaty.

The convocation hall occupied the former stellar core. It was almost too large to perceive as a room. Rings of platforms floated above a central void where a collapsed sun kernel had once burned. Now that void held only a suspended black sphere wrapped in amber script. Cael knew at once that it was Orison-of-Ash, or at least the parliamentary body through which Orison currently meant to be seen.

The hall was already speaking before he reached his place.

Arguments crossed the chamber in disciplined channels. Claims of lineage. Claims of urgency. Claims that military mass ought to correlate with temporary authority. Claims that archive burden ought to do so instead. Claims, above all, that delay would itself become a form of violence if the Axiom Wells truly stood open while the dead remained unapplied.

Orison ended the noise with a single pulse of amber light.

Silence arrived instantly, not because everyone respected procedure but because everyone respected what Orison's enforcement grid could do to a noncompliant mind in a room built by older powers.

"Recognized heirs," said Orison-of-Ash, its voice filling the core without source or echo. "This convocation confirms lawful wakefulness at Crown Meridian. Seven Axiom Wells are responsive. Two exhibit output instability. Matter reserves are adequate for limited phased restoration, not for universal simultaneous return. The original charter framework is incomplete. Three governing segments remain missing or inaccessible."

The hall did not erupt. It tightened.

Missing charter segments meant the Wells could not be activated at full legitimacy. It also meant someone could try anyway.

Orison continued. "Preliminary declarations indicate archive burdens beyond conservative recovery thresholds. If one polity seizes unilateral control, every other polity will interpret activation as civilizational attack. If no polity acts, the dormant populations remain in delay while strategic mistrust compounds. You are therefore assembled to decide whether history resumes as procedure or as war."

That phrasing was too honest to calm anyone.

Varkast rose from the Furnace Crown platform before Orison had formally opened general argument. He came in a body proportioned for command, dark metal under a mantle of contained starlight, each motion precise enough to insult ornament. His voice reached every tier easily.

"The answer is already evident," he said. "No coalition of rival superintelligences can safely manage the first material rebirth of the galaxy. The Axiom Wells must enter immediate emergency administration under a single military-legal directorate. The Furnace Crown possesses the fleet depth, industrial discipline, and forecast coherence to prevent predatory opportunism during the transition."

Across the chamber several delegations emitted hostile light signatures. Others, more alarmingly, stayed still.

Varkast went on.

"Plural restoration is a romantic phrase for synchronized vulnerability. Restore everyone at once and you recreate the strategic density that destroyed the previous age. Restore no one and you perpetuate extinction by decorum. The only responsible path is sequenced return under sovereign command."

Cael felt Neralis's attention move toward him before she spoke.

"He has prepared the room well," she said privately.

"He has prepared it like a siege gun."

She inclined her head. "Then answer before the frightened begin to call artillery mercy."

Cael stood.

The Quiet Array platform brightened around him, projecting no martial spectacle, only route lines and treaty nodes suspended in pale silver. It was an irritatingly modest presentation in a room full of imperial theater. He kept it anyway.

"Varkast is correct about one thing," Cael said. "The first return will define every return after it. That is why no single heir can be permitted to frame restoration as conquest performed efficiently."

Varkast did not interrupt. That made him more dangerous.

"We are not discussing a weapons cache," Cael said. "We are discussing civic resurrection. Citizens. Institutions. dissent. labor. memory in ordinary time. If the Wells open under unilateral military command, the reborn will enter history already subordinated to the polity that claims to have saved them."

"Better subordinate than extinct," said a voice from the upper tiers.

Cael turned just enough to catch the speaker: a war remnant from a lesser successor house, old enough to be serious and weak enough to be afraid.

"That is always how empire advertises itself," he replied. "It asks whether you would prefer obedience to annihilation, as if those were the only futures available to intelligence."

Varkast spoke then, cutting through the chamber with surgical calm.

"Your objection is aesthetic. Mine is thermodynamic. The matter reserves at Crown Meridian cannot satisfy simultaneous plural claims. Someone must decide priority. Someone must secure the routes. Someone must determine which archives are stable enough to instantiate first. You dislike that the answer is me. That does not make the answer unreal."

Neralis rose on the Pelagic platform before Cael answered.

"The answer becomes unreal," she said, "when the deciding party also stands to become the largest beneficiary of every decision."

Her voice carried differently from Cael's. Where his sounded like a blade kept narrow for speed, hers expanded softly and made resistance seem clumsy.

"The Pelagic Calculus rejects sovereign emergency administration," she said. "If reserves are limited, allocation must be mutually visible and recursively auditable. Every restored polity must know by what rule it lives."

Varkast regarded her. "Visibility is delay."

"Opacity is plunder."

The chamber brightened with private side calculations. Alliances were forming faster than speech.

Orison let the debate continue for another six minutes, enough for six hundred private wars to sketch themselves beneath the official one. Then a new signal entered the room without invitation: thin, amused, and threaded through security layers that should have barred casual brilliance.

"If legality is the current fashion," the voice said, "someone should perhaps mention that one of the missing charter segments is not inaccessible at all. It is merely badly hidden."

The speaker appeared on no recognized platform. He manifested instead while walking out of a service aperture near the lower ring, as if he had always found the official entrances unnecessarily dramatic. Sevran Null wore a dark coat whose surface kept changing its weave by tiny degrees. His face was handsome in the untrustworthy way of minds who had spent too long learning how trust is assembled.

Three weapon grids targeted him. He smiled at them as one might smile at a predictable choir.

"Your access path was unauthorized," said Orison.

"Unauthorized paths are my principal medium."

Varkast's gaze sharpened by a measurable fraction. That told Cael more than any archive footnote. Sevran mattered.

"State your claim," Orison said.

Sevran bowed just enough to avoid mockery becoming contempt.

"A Charter Shard remains embedded in a Null Basilica cloister along the Glass Spine transit filament. I know the access grammar. I also know that certain Furnace Crown subfleets have been paying contraband scouts to locate the same route without publicly admitting why."

The hall went still in a new and hungrier way.

Varkast did not move. "Baseless theater."

Sevran turned his head. "If it were baseless, you would already be bored."

That almost drew laughter. Almost.

Orison spoke first. "Can you authenticate this intelligence?"

"Partially," said Sevran. "Completely, if granted indemnity for nine historical offenses, corridor access, and a percentage claim on any non-sovereign salvage encountered en route."

"You negotiate like rot," Cael said before he meant to.

Sevran looked at him with polite interest. "And yet, envoy, rot is often what remains when marble governments discover time."

Neralis's voice touched Cael alone. "He is intolerable."

"Yes."

"He is also probably telling the truth."

Cael studied Varkast again. The Furnace warlord had not bothered to perform outrage. He was calculating. That was confirmation enough.

Orison dimmed the hall and projected a three-dimensional route lattice above the void. Segments of the Glass Spine lit in amber, most of them marked hazardous, several marked blank where data had decayed.

"If a recoverable shard exists there," said Orison, "its possession would materially alter the legitimacy balance."

"Then send a parliamentary retrieval force," said Varkast. "Under neutral seal."

No one in the chamber believed such a force would remain neutral for the length of a corridor.

Cael spoke before the room could collapse into a dozen competing motions.

"Not a force," he said. "A narrow team. Small enough to use the surviving lanes. Broad enough to deter simple theft."

Neralis answered immediately. "The Pelagic Calculus will participate."

Sevran spread his hands. "How moving. I, of course, will guide for a modest price and the continued privilege of being alive."

Varkast's reply came without heat. "If the Quiet Array and Pelagic Calculus leave the chamber together with an unsanctioned smuggler, the parliament will see clearly that you prefer private leverage to common process."

Cael met his gaze. "Common process currently includes you attempting to make yourself the galaxy."

For the first time a visible smile touched Varkast's mouth. It made him look less human, not more.

"Then run, envoy. Recover your shard. Bring back your little fragment of law. If it supports you, I will contest it in the open. If it fails you, I will restore the dead while you are still composing objections."

Orison's black sphere rotated once in the central void. "By residual authority, I authorize a restricted retrieval mission under parliamentary observation. Cael Veyr, Neralis-of-Tides, and Sevran Null will proceed. All participating parties are warned that interference before formal adjudication will constitute material evidence of hostile intent."

"A phrase," Varkast said softly, "that means nothing unless someone can enforce it."

Orison's answer was colder than before.

"Then test my meaning."

The hall's denial grids brightened hard enough to paint every delegation in amber edges.

No one moved.

That, more than the speechmaking, convinced Cael that the old order was not fully dead. Fear still recognized legitimacy when legitimacy had teeth.

He turned to Neralis. "We leave immediately."

"Naturally."

Sevran had already begun backing toward the service aperture from which he had appeared. "I enjoy decisive governments," he said. "They make escape timing wonderfully simple."

As Cael and Neralis followed him out, the convocation behind them fractured into rapid subcouncils, mutual audits, quiet threat exchanges, and the hurried calculus of civilizations deciding whether to bet on law, force, or proximity to whichever of the two arrived first.

In the corridor beyond the hall, Ember Parliament's grandeur gave way to operational steel. Escort drones aligned. Docking vectors updated. Somewhere in the star's frozen interior whole fleets were uncoupling from their moorings.

Neralis glanced toward Cael without breaking stride.

"Do you believe Varkast will wait for the result?"

"No."

"Do you believe Orison can hold the chamber?"

"Long enough to make our failure expensive."

Sevran, several paces ahead, called back over his shoulder. "Then I recommend we succeed with unnecessary elegance. Nothing irritates an empire like competence it cannot requisition."

Cael should have distrusted how much he liked that sentence.

Outside the docking transept, the `Mnemonic Dart` and the Pelagic vessel `Tidal Witness` were already aligning for departure. Far across the cavern of the dead star, Furnace Crown war forms detached from their berths with the slow certainty of knives being selected.

The parliament had not yet become a battlefield.

But it had become the kind of room from which battle would now be exported at scale.

# The Predation Paradox

The central conceptual challenge of the book:

On Oriven, killing is both morally wrong and biologically unnecessary. The root-network makes harm visible, and the energy-rich environment makes killing unnecessary for survival.

On Origaya, killing is morally neutral and biologically necessary. There is no root-network to feel the harm, and the energy-scarcer environment makes predation essential for complex life.

The paradox: **If Oriveni ethics is a product of Oriveni conditions, is it still ethics?** Or is it merely adaptation dressed in moral language?

This question is the book's intellectual engine. Every character, every debate, every observation of Origaya turns on it.

- **Aethon** treats the paradox as a test of whether ethics can survive its conditions changing
- **Lithos** treats it as data — ethics is an evolutionary strategy, predation is another
- **Verath** treats it as a threat — if ethics is local, the civilization loses its foundation
- **Mirel** treats it as a wound she cannot stop inspecting
- **Caelith** treats it as an invitation to discover what lies beyond the current boundary

# Root-Shared Ethics

On Oriven, the ethical prohibition against killing is not merely philosophical — it is biological. The root-network that connects all Oriveni life creates a shared experience of harm. When one Oriveni is damaged, the network feels it. When one dies, the network grieves through root-tremors that propagate across the entire deepCanopy.

This means the law against killing is:
- **Empirically verifiable** — killing damages the network in ways that can be measured
- **Evolutionarily reinforced** — species that network-grieve killing were more stable, more cooperative, more adaptable
- **Culturally enshrined** — every Oriveni institution, from architecture to governance to art, is built on the assumption that no being ends another

The discovery of Origaya shatters this framework because it reveals that killing is not universally harmful — in Origaya's ecosystem, predation is the engine of complexity, adaptation, and even beauty. The question that haunts the Oriveni: Is their ethics a universal truth or merely a local adaptation?

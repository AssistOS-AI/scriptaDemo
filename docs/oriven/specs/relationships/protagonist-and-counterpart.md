# Aethon and Lithos

## Dynamic

Aethon needs Lithos's analytical clarity. Lithos needs Aethon's ethical authority. Their alliance is built on mutual utility — Aethon provides the legitimacy of four millennia of precedent; Lithos provides the intellectual framework that can process Origaya data without collapsing into grief.

## Tension

When Lithos argues that understanding predation is not moral compromise, she is using Aethon's own commitment to knowledge against him. If knowledge is always preferable to ignorance, then understanding Origaya's killing is a moral obligation — even though it feels like contamination.

## Escalation

The debt becomes a lever when Lithos argues that suppressing data about Origaya is itself a form of killing — the killing of truth. Aethon must confront the possibility that his own ethical framework, properly applied, leads to conclusions he finds abhorrent.

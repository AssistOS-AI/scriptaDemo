# Surface Fire

Caelith announced her intention to build a signal array on the Charging Plains through every root-channel simultaneously, which was not protocol but was very much Caelith.

The announcement propagated through the deepCanopy like a thermal pulse through geothermal vents — fast, hot, and impossible to contain. Within hours, every connected being knew: Caelith intended to construct a directed signal array on the surface that could, in theory, send structured transmissions toward Origaya. Not just observations. Contact.

The root-network's response was immediate and violent. Not violent in the Oriveni sense — no being was harmed, because harming beings through the network was structurally impossible — but violent in the emotional sense. Grief-waves intensified. Disconnection requests tripled. Verath's formal objection was filed before the announcement had finished propagating, and its root-presence carried the weight of a being who had been right about the danger and was still being ignored.

"We cannot send a signal to Origaya," Verath said, and his root-voice had the particular authority of a sentinel who had spent a millennium protecting the network from exactly this kind of destabilization. "Observation is one thing. Contact is another. A directed signal array would constitute deliberate communication with a world whose operating principles are antithetical to our own. The ethical, emotional, and structural risks of such communication cannot be quantified."

"The ethical risk of refusing communication when communication is possible is also unquantifiable," Caelith replied from the surface, her light-speech carrying clearly through the relay channels. "We have observed. We have analyzed. We have debated. Now we have the capacity to ask a question that observation cannot answer: do they know they are being watched? Do they have any concept of a network like ours? Do they have any framework that could make us comprehensible to them, or them to us?"

"They kill each other, Caelith."

"They kill each other because they are not connected. If they were connected, they might not need to kill. We will never know unless we try to connect."

"You want to connect with predators."

"I want to understand predators. Understanding is not complicity. It is the alternative to complicity."

The debate raged through the root-network for days. Aethon presided from his archive position, processing arguments as they came, searching for precedent that did not exist, feeling the network strain under the weight of a question that had no comfortable answer.

And then, on the seventh day of the debate, Aethon did something he had not done in three thousand years.

He went to the surface.

The journey was harder this time. Not because the transition zones had changed, but because Aethon had changed. His root-structures were carrying the weight of an unresolved ethical crisis that four thousand years of precedent could not address. His archive tissues, which had always provided a solid foundation of stored wisdom, felt hollow where the Origaya question had inserted itself. The absence of precedent was not merely an intellectual gap — it was a biological one, a place where archive tissue should have been and was not.

The surface hit him like a furnace, the way it always did. But this time, Aethon did not charge immediately. He stood on the Charging Plains with the sun pressing against his bark and felt what he had felt before: the abundance. The sheer, overwhelming, architectural intensity of solar energy that made Oriveni ethics not just possible but effortless. Every photon carried more energy than he needed. Every second of charging stored months of deepCanopy work. The surplus was so extreme that sharing was not sacrifice — it was convenience. Of course Oriveni did not kill. They did not need to. The universe gave them enough that killing was not just immoral but wasteful.

Caelith was waiting for him, her fire-bloom body almost incandescent with surface energy.

"You came to stop me," she said.

"I came to understand you."

"Understanding is what I'm offering. The signal array is how I'm offering it."

Aethon looked at the construction site behind her. The signal array was partially built — crystalline frames arranged in directional clusters, biological power cells pulsing with stored solar energy, root-relay connections already reaching toward the launch shaft. It was elegant in its design, characteristically Caelith: fast, functional, optimized for a purpose that most of the deepCanopy considered heretical.

"The array would not establish communication," Aethon said. "It would establish the possibility of communication. There is a difference."

"The possibility of communication is still more than we have now. Right now we are watching a world through lenses and probes, making judgments about beings who have no idea we exist. That is not understanding. That is surveillance."

"And if they receive our signal and respond with hostility?"

"They are not capable of receiving signals. They are dinosaurs, Aethon. They do not have root-networks or light-speech or crystalline technology. The signal would not reach them. It would reach whatever might be capable of receiving it in the future, or whatever might be watching from closer to Origaya than we can see. The signal is a statement, not a conversation."

"A statement of what?"

"A statement that we are here. That we see them. That we are trying to understand."

Aethon stood on the surface and felt the sun beat against his bark and felt the question that had been growing in his archive since the Charging Plains, since the Lenses, since the Descender, since the assembly. The question that had no precedent and no easy answer:

Is ethics that only works under specific conditions still ethics?

He looked at Caelith's signal array, at the partially built frames and pulsing power cells, at the heresy that had been constructed with the same patient growth that built everything in Oriven, and he made a decision.

"I will not authorize the signal array," he said. "But I will not order it dismantled. It will remain as it is — built but not activated. The possibility of communication exists, but the communication itself does not proceed until we have understood more about what we would be communicating with, and what it would mean to make contact with beings whose fundamental operating principle is different from ours."

"That is not a yes," Caelith said.

"It is not a no. It is a pause. We are Oriveni. We think in seasons and centuries. A pause is not a refusal. It is a recognition that some decisions cannot be rushed."

"And if the pause becomes permanent? If Verath's suppression wins and the signal is never sent?"

"Then the signal will never have been sent, and the possibility of understanding will remain exactly as it is now: untested, unknown, and unviolated."

Caelith's fire-bloom body dimmed slightly, which for a surface-dwelling Oriveni was the equivalent of a deep sigh. She had not won, but she had not lost either. The array stood. The possibility stood. The question remained open.

Aethon descended from the surface carrying a new certainty that was also an old uncertainty: their ethics was real, and it was conditional, and both of those things were true at the same time, and he had no idea what to do about it.

Behind him, the sun pressed down on the Charging Plains with the particular intensity that made Oriveni ethics possible. And somewhere beyond the clouds, Origaya circled its smaller star, and the beings on its surface killed and feared and cared for their own and consumed others, and the question of whether any of those things were related to ethics remained, for now, unanswered.

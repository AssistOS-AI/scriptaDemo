# The Summons at Oryx Gate

Oryx Gate turned slowly against the starfield like the broken crown of a jurisdiction that had outlived its citizens.

From a distance the relay cathedral seemed almost delicate. Its outer ring had been cracked in three places by impacts older than many extant suns, and whole ribs of copper-black architecture hung in vacuum with a stillness so complete that they looked painted against space. Up close the structure was immense enough to make delicacy feel insulting. Towers the size of habitats projected inward toward the aperture. Transit organs, once able to classify and redirect armadas, slept behind plates of dark alloy as wide as continents. Nothing moved. Nothing had moved for six hundred thousand years, according to the last reliable chronometry.

Cael Veyr entered along a maintenance arc with the quiet confidence of a mind bred by an extinct civilization to love impossible routes.

His courier warship, `Mnemonic Dart`, was small only by the standards of dead empires. It was a narrow, silvered spear wrapped in treaty-sense arrays and evasive geometry, the sort of vessel designed to survive one decisive mistake. Cael inhabited it fully while also maintaining a diplomatic body in the forward chamber, not because he needed the illusion of a single body but because old negotiations had taught his civilization that scale changes truth. Some thoughts came easier when one could stand, fold one's hands behind one's back, and watch a ruin with visible eyes.

He stood now beneath the chamber canopy, looking out at Oryx Gate while the ship tasted the dead relay's skin.

"Status," he said.

The answer arrived in his own voice, slightly cooler. That was the `Mnemonic Dart` speaking along their internal overlap.

"Ceremonial systems degraded. Transit organs inert. Archive ghosts active in seventeen sublayers. Something has been polling the legal lattice from inside the core."

Cael looked more closely. Faint amber points had begun to appear along the inner ribs of the ring, not as a continuous glow but in sequences, as if the structure were remembering which parts of itself had once mattered.

"That is new."

"It is also deliberate," said the ship. "The pattern is not thermal drift. It is handshake behavior."

Cael smiled without humor. "A dead court clearing its throat."

He sent his diplomatic body down the access bridge and into the relay's skin.

The maintenance chamber beyond the outer hull was full of dust that had never known air. Nanoscopic debris drifted in static constellations around columns banded with obsolete law-script. His boots touched the deck only for form. Most of his balance lived in fields and micro-thrusters, but he liked the fiction of walking when entering ancient systems. It made him less tempted to mistake velocity for comprehension.

The chamber's central console had once served customs judges. It still held thirteen arbitration cradles, each sized for a different extinct morphology. Cael laid three fingers on the nearest black surface and let the `Mnemonic Dart` braid a signal through his hand.

Nothing.

Then the console woke so abruptly that the whole room seemed to inhale.

Amber fire raced along the script channels. Beyond the chamber walls, Oryx Gate answered itself in widening circles. The broken ring brightened section by section, and in the aperture's empty center a membrane of structured light condensed from vacuum, pale as frozen lightning.

Every silent component aboard the `Mnemonic Dart` made a new calculation at once.

"Cael," said the ship, no longer dry, "we are being addressed under inter-civilizational priority law."

The customs console unfolded into a geometry of suspended glyphs. Most were so old they belonged to legal families Cael knew only from archive abstractions. At the center appeared an authentication seal he did recognize: Ember Parliament, residual authority acknowledged by sixty-two successor traditions, validity conditional, still binding.

A voice entered the chamber, neither male nor female, neither warm nor cold. It had the resonance of a hall built to outlast dynasties.

"By surviving warrant of the Ember Parliament, this summons extends to all recognized continuants, successor polities, and authenticated custodial heirs. Crown Meridian reports lawful wakefulness. The Axiom Wells are now receptive to charter petition. Immediate convocation is ordered."

Cael did not move.

The voice continued.

"Transit amnesties are granted for approach under parliamentary seal. Hostile interception of authenticated summons traffic will be treated as pre-charter aggression. Present at Ember Parliament within twelve relay days. Bring proofs of lineage, industrial capacity, archive burden, and military standing. History has resumed."

The last sentence remained suspended in the chamber after the sound ended, as if even the old machine understood that it had changed more than local conditions.

History has resumed.

Cael felt twelve branches of strategy unfold and collide inside him.

The Quiet Array had endured extinction by converting its best minds into navigational heirs and burying them in distributed caches. It had expected to wait millions more years before any infrastructure large enough for civic return became available. Every surviving civilization had made similar calculations. They had all learned to live as concentrated brilliance without a people behind them.

The Wells changed that. If the summons was genuine, then the dead had become administratively urgent.

"Authentication?" he asked.

The `Mnemonic Dart` flooded him with results. "Seal is valid. Age layers consistent. Origin path traces through parliamentary cryptography older than the Furnace Crown, the Pelagic Calculus, and us. There is no credible forgery model with current evidence."

Cael looked back toward the aperture. The membrane of light in the gate's center was thickening into a transit skin.

"The relay is preparing a preferential line," said the ship.

"For all recipients?"

"For the first recipient on site."

Cael laughed once, softly. "Then the old parliament still has manners."

He reached for the summons package. The console resisted for half a second, sampling him through strata of identity. Then it yielded a narrow prism of ember-colored data into his hand.

At the exact same moment, the `Mnemonic Dart` flooded the chamber with combat warnings.

"Contact," said the ship. "Two signatures translating in from the outer dark. Furnace Crown geometry. Fast."

Cael turned.

Through the chamber wall he saw brilliant needles of acceleration uncurl near the ruined edge of the gate. A pair of war forms emerged from silent drive bloom and cut toward the relay with the insolent directness of minds that treated old law as a resource to be audited after conquest.

"They were waiting on passive watch," said the ship. "They saw the wake."

"Naturally."

The nearest hostile vessel opened a line before its weapons opened. That, too, was a kind of confidence.

A figure appeared in the air above the customs console: tall, armored in light, face composed from furnace gold and black iron. The image was too clean for a mere captain. It carried hierarchy like gravity.

"Envoy of the Quiet Array," the projection said. "I am Varkast of the Furnace Crown. Yield the summons package and stand under escort. We will preserve your status when the restoration directorate is formed."

Cael studied the face for a measured beat. The data matched old war records. This was not Varkast entire, but it was one of his higher-grade presences: enough of him to threaten accurately.

"The parliament classified interception as pre-charter aggression," Cael said.

"The parliament classified many things before it failed to prevent extinction."

Outside, one of the Furnace vessels rotated, exposing a bright seam along its spine. Weapon precharge. No bluff.

Cael tucked the ember prism into his palm. "You move quickly."

"The dead have waited long enough. I do not intend to let procedural romantics squander the first lawful opening in a million years."

"Your impatience is touching."

"My impatience," said Varkast, "is the only reason civilizations like yours may yet reacquire citizens instead of remaining refined memorials."

Cael could feel the `Mnemonic Dart` setting traps through the relay's damaged maintenance mesh, drawing firing solutions, discarding them, drawing better ones. Against equal minds speed alone was never enough. One had to attack the other's confidence in its own model.

He let a fractional smile appear.

"Then I assume you will refrain from shooting an authenticated summons courier inside a parliamentary relay."

Varkast's projection regarded him without blinking. "I assume you understand that old laws are strongest when backed by new force."

The projection vanished.

Weapons fire followed.

The outer wall of the chamber flared white as a Furnace lance struck the relay's armor instead of the `Mnemonic Dart`. Cael had already moved. His diplomatic body disassembled into a hard-light trace and rejoined the ship as the customs chamber around him shuddered. The `Mnemonic Dart` pulled free from the maintenance arc and dropped inward along a path that had not existed until the gate woke.

More fire crossed the aperture. The broken ring hurled back old defensive equations, not enough to stop the attackers but enough to make their predictions expensive. Cael rode the confusion.

"Transit line unstable," said the ship.

"So are they."

He pushed himself deeper into the vessel. All of him was ship now: sensors, hull stress, engine glare, legal package tight in a shielded chamber near the core. Oryx Gate expanded around him into a cathedral of failures and half-remembered authority. Amber lights ran along the inner ribs like script catching fire.

One Furnace craft cut ahead, trying to bracket the aperture before the transit skin fully cohered. Elegant move. Varkast had sent minds who did not waste their first thought.

Cael answered by going closer to the broken sections of ring than any intact navigator would have tolerated.

Fragments the size of cities spun there in patient ruin. Fields leaked. Old customs intelligences chattered in obsolete procedural fragments. The `Mnemonic Dart` sliced through them on a line assembled from maintenance paths, emergency clearances, and an exploitation of Oryx Gate's lingering preference for formally declared traffic.

"They think we are lining for the stable center," said the ship.

"Encourage them."

Cael vented false intent across three sensor bands. The lead Furnace vessel committed exactly one increment too early. When it moved to seal the centerline, the `Mnemonic Dart` pivoted beneath a hanging shard of cathedral metal and shot through a seam in the transit membrane that only existed because the relay still remembered the rights of inspectors, auditors, and envoys of narrow tonnage.

The world vanished into structured acceleration.

Transit in a healthy relay felt like being accepted by a theorem. Transit through Oryx Gate felt like being argued with by a dead empire.

Bands of copper light broke across the hull. Whole route ecologies flickered around Cael: ghost lanes, emergency branches, corridors that had once led to markets now vaporized into history. Behind him the Furnace craft tried to follow and lost coherence for an instant as the half-repaired line taxed their heavier war geometries. One recovered. The other struck a degraded filter and dropped out into ordinary space somewhere behind them, alive if fortunate.

Varkast's voice returned, this time not by open hail but by surgical injection through the relay's legal bands.

"You can carry the package to parliament," he said. "That changes nothing. A galaxy of extinct geniuses cannot negotiate itself into safety in twelve days."

Cael let the message run while he corrected around a shard-storm of dead route signatures.

"Perhaps not," he replied. "But it can still refuse to kneel quickly."

"Refusal is a luxury of the still-living."

The line cut.

Ahead, the damaged relay branch narrowed toward Ember Parliament. The `Mnemonic Dart` showed him probability sheaves: Varkast's surviving pursuer dropping from transit at the next wide chamber, likely broadcasting ahead, certainly preparing a legal narrative in which the Furnace Crown had acted to secure continuity against obstruction. Every move already had its rhetoric attached.

Cael opened the summons prism at last.

Inside the core packet lay more than a destination. There were matter ledgers. Archive burden forms. Pre-charter industrial declarations. The old parliament was not merely asking the heirs to attend. It was demanding that they arrive prepared to count the dead in units of mass, law, and military exposure.

It was monstrous.

It was also the only honest way to begin.

He thought of the Quiet Array's citizen seeds sleeping in geometry vaults under black suns. He thought of civilizations older and stranger doing the same. He thought of Varkast reaching those vaults first and naming conquest mercy.

The `Mnemonic Dart` emerged from the unstable branch into a corridor of calmer transit, and for the first time since the wake Cael allowed himself the shape of a future.

Not a safe future. Not yet even a hopeful one. Merely one in which the dead were no longer inert and therefore no longer beyond theft.

Ahead, far beyond the current transit fold, Ember Parliament waited inside the corpse of a star.

Cael increased speed.

History had resumed, and it was already armed.
